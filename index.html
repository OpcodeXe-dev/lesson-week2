Lesson Proper for Week 1

4.1 Why Do Research?

  • Research Teaches Methods of Discovery. Explanation on a topic prompts you to discover what you know on a topic and what others can teach you...
  • Research Teaches Investigative Skills. A research project requires you to investigate a subject, gain a grasp of its essentials, and disclose your findings...
  • Research Develops Inquiry-Based Techniques. With the guidance of your instructor, you are making inquiry...
  • Research Builds Career Skills. Many career fields rely on investigation and inquiry for fact-finding purposes...
  • Research Teaches Critical Thinking. As you wade through the evidence on your subject, you will learn to discriminate between useful information and unfounded comments.
  • Research Teaches Logic. Like a judge in the courtroom, you must make perceptive judgments...
  • Research Teaches the Basic Ingredients of Argument. In most cases, a research paper requires you to make a claim and support it with reasons and evidence...

4.2 Learning the Conventions of Academic Writing

Researched writing in each discipline follows certain conventions—special forms for citing sources and designing pages...

  • Analysis - Classify the major issues of your study and provide detailed analysis of each in defense of your thesis.
  • Evidence - Provide well-reasoned propositions and statements supported by facts.
  • Discussion - Relate the implications of your findings and the merits of the study.

4.3 Understanding and Avoiding Plagiarism

Plagiarism is defined as the act of claiming the words or ideas of another person as your own...

Avoiding Unintentional Plagiarism

  • Citation – Introduce borrowed content with the author's name.
  • Quotation marks – Use for all direct quotes.
  • Paraphrase – Cite the source of paraphrased ideas.
  • Parenthetical citations and notes – Follow appropriate style guides (MLA, APA, etc.).
  • Works cited or references pages – List all sources in the correct format.

4.4 Understanding the Terminology

Evaluation

To evaluate, you first need to establish clear criteria of judgment and then explain how the subject meets these criteria...

Interpretation

To interpret, you must usually answer, “What does it mean?”...

Definition

Sometimes you will need to provide an extended definition to show that your subject fits into a selected and well-defined category...

Proposal

A proposal says to the reader, “We should do something.” It often has practical applications...

Causal Argument

Causal arguments show that a condition exists because of specific circumstances...

Comparison, Including Analogy

An argument often compares and likens a subject to something else...

Precedence

Precedence refers to conventions or customs, usually well established...

Implications

At some point, you will be expected to explain your findings, arrive at conclusions, and discuss the implications of your scientific inquiry...
