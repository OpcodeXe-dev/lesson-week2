Week 2 Lesson: Personal Ideas & Scholarly Problems

Key Takeaways

Week 2's lesson focuses on a crucial skill for academic writing: connecting your personal interests and experiences to scholarly problems and academic disciplines. It emphasizes that while you can't submit a personal essay as a research paper, your personal connection can be the springboard for a compelling scholarly inquiry.

Bridging Personal & Academic

The core idea is to find a scholarly problem that resonates with your personal experiences or interests. For example, a student whose mother struggled with internet addiction could explore the issue academically, or a personal interest in skiing could lead to research in sports medicine.

Idea Generation Techniques

  • Free writing: Writing continuously on a topic to uncover thoughts and connections.

  • Listing keywords: Identifying fundamental terms in relevant literature to focus your research.

  • Narrowing by comparison: Examining differences between two related concepts, people, or groups.

  • Asking questions: Formulating research questions your paper will aim to answer.

  • Talking with others: Discussing your ideas with experts, classmates, family, or online communities.

Leveraging Resources for Topic Refinement

  • Online Subject Directories: These help you broaden or narrow your initial topic by showing related subtopics (e.g., "alternative medicine" to "aromatherapy").

  • Google Scholar: A specialized search engine for academic literature like articles, theses, and books from scholarly publishers and universities.

  • Library Databases (e.g., InfoTrac, ERIC, ProQuest): These provide access to peer-reviewed articles and reliable sources, offering a more curated and trustworthy alternative to general internet searches.

  • Library's Electronic Book Catalog: Useful for finding in-depth studies and confirming if your chosen subject has been explored in books.

Developing a Controlling Idea

Once you have a focused topic, you'll need a central statement to guide your research:

  • Thesis Statement: A conclusion you will defend (e.g., "Human beings have always participated in wars").

  • Enthymeme: A claim with a "because" clause to be defended (e.g., "There has never been a 'noble savage' because even prehistoric humans fought wars").

  • Hypothesis: A theory to be tested through research (e.g., "Human beings are motivated by biological instincts toward the physical overthrow of perceived enemies").

Crafting a Research Proposal

This can be a short paragraph or a multi-page report.

Short Proposal:

  • Specific topic
  • Purpose of the paper (to explain, analyze, or argue)
  • Intended audience (general or specialized)
  • Your voice as the writer (informer or advocate)
  • Preliminary thesis statement or opening hypothesis

Long Proposal (may include):

  • Cover page, abstract
  • Purpose statement with rationale
  • Statement of your qualifications
  • Literature review
  • Description of research methods

Understanding Your Purpose and Audience

Research papers typically aim to explain, analyze, or persuade. It's crucial to consider your audience's expertise and expectations, tailoring your language and approach accordingly.

By effectively connecting your personal curiosity with established academic fields and utilizing available resources, you can transform an initial idea into a well-defined and researchable scholarly problem.
