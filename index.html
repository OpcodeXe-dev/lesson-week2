Lesson Proper for Week 2

Relating Your Personal Ideas to a Scholarly Problem

Try to make a connection between your interests and the inherent issues of the subject. For instance, a student whose mother became seriously addicted to the Internet developed a paper from the personal experiences of her dysfunctional family.

  • Relate your experiences to scholarly problems and academic disciplines.
  • Speculate about the subject by listing issues, asking questions, engaging in free writing, and using other idea-generating techniques.

Connecting Personal Experience to Scholarly Topics

You can’t write a personal essay and call it a research paper, yet you can choose topics close to your life. Use one of the techniques described in the following list:

1. Combine personal interests with academic studies:

  • Personal interest: Skiing
  • Academic subject: Sports medicine
  • Possible topics: “Protecting the Knees”, “Therapy for Strained Muscles”, “Skin Treatments”

2. Consider social issues that affect you and your family:

  • Personal interest: The education of my child
  • Social issue: Child's behavior in school
  • Possible topics: “Children Who Are Hyperactive”, “Should Schoolchildren Take Medicine to Calm Their Hyperactivity?”

3. Consider scientific subjects, if appropriate:

  • Personal interest: Farm water sources
  • Scientific subject: Water toxins
  • Possible topic: “The Poisoning of Underground Water Tables”

4. Let your cultural background prompt detailed research:

  • Background: Native American — “The Indian Wars from the Native American’s Point of View”
  • Background: Hispanic — “Bicultural Experiences of Hispanic Students: The Failures and Triumphs”

Speculating about Your Subject

Ideas can be generated in the following ways:

  • Free Writing: Develop ideas by writing nonstop.
  • Keyword Listing: Focus research by listing terms from sources.
  • Narrowing by Comparison: Compare people or groups to find focus.
  • Asking Questions: Formulate clear research questions.
  • Talking with Others: Discuss your subject for fresh insights.

Using Online Searches to Refine Your Topic

Use tools like Google Scholar, InfoTrac, ERIC, ProQuest, and EBSCOhost for academic results.

  • Use quotation marks for exact phrases.
  • Stick to peer-reviewed sources.
  • Use your school’s ebook catalog for background reading.

Developing a Thesis Statement, Enthymeme, or Hypothesis

  • Thesis Statement: A conclusion you'll defend.
  • Enthymeme: A claim + reason (“because” statement).
  • Hypothesis: A theory tested through research.

Drafting a Research Proposal

Short Proposal

  • Specific topic
  • Purpose (explanation, analysis, or argument)
  • Intended audience
  • Writer’s role (informer or advocate)
  • Thesis or hypothesis

Long Proposal

  • Cover page with details
  • Abstract (50–100 words)
  • Purpose statement and rationale
  • Statement of qualification
  • Literature review
  • Method, timeline, and budget (if applicable)

Explaining Your Purpose in the Research Proposal

  • Explanation: Present facts and clarify the subject.
  • Analysis: Break the subject into parts and examine them.
  • Persuasion: Argue for a position or solution.
